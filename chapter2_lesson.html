The Investigator's Office & Laboratory

Setting up a digital forensics lab — from accreditation and physical security to workstation selection and building a business case.

A digital forensics lab is where you conduct investigations, store evidence, and house your equipment, hardware, and software. It is the central hub of all forensic work.

The ANSI-ASQ National Accreditation Board (ANAB) provides accreditation of crime and forensics labs worldwide, including those that analyze digital evidence. To earn accreditation, ANAB audits a lab's tasks and functions to ensure correct and consistent results for all cases.

Why Accreditation Matters: Accreditation ensures your forensics lab follows standardized procedures, which strengthens the credibility of your findings in court and across jurisdictions.

Lab Manager Duties: The lab manager sets up goals, schedules, staff duties, and responsibilities. They assign cases and establish expectations for preliminary and final reports.

Staff Responsibilities: Staff members must maintain current knowledge and seek training programs for professional development. Staying aware of new technologies is essential.

Budget Planning

Break costs into monthly, quarterly, and annual expenses. Use past investigation expenses to extrapolate expected future costs. Key budget items include:

Purchasing hardware and software, renting facility space, and training personnel. The lab manager is responsible for overall budget planning.

Tip: Review a Uniform Crime Report to understand crime statistics when planning your budget. If most computers in your jurisdiction use a particular OS, your lab should be equipped to handle cases on that platform.

For private companies, lab setup requires evaluating hardware and software inventory, problems reported in the previous year, and anticipated future developments in computing technology. Time management is a major factor when choosing what to purchase.

Appropriate training and certification provide credibility and demonstrate competency. However, certification programs can be expensive and requirements may be difficult to meet.

Key Certification Programs

IACIS — International Association of Computer Investigative Specialists, offering the CFCE (Certified Forensic Computer Examiner).

ISC² — Offers the CCFP (Certified Cyber Forensics Professional) for those with education and work experience.

HTCN — High Tech Crime Network, providing certification for computer crime investigators and forensics technicians.

Other notable programs include: EnCE (EnCase Certified Examiner), ACE (AccessData Certified Examiner), EC-Council, SANS Institute, DCITA (Defense Cyber Investigations Training Academy), ISFCE for the CCE certification, CTIN, DFCB, CSA (Cloud Security Alliance), FLETC, and NW3C (National White Collar Crime Center).

Find the certification program that best fits your career goals, budget, and experience level.

Most investigative work is performed in the lab, so it must provide a safe and secure physical environment for both personnel and evidence. Maintain inventory controls of all assets to know when to reorder supplies.

Minimum Lab Security Requirements

A forensics lab is a secure facility oriented to preserve the integrity of evidence. The minimum requirements include:

A small room with true floor-to-ceiling walls, door access with a locking mechanism, a secure container (safe or heavy-duty file cabinet with a quality padlock), and a visitor's log listing everyone who has accessed the lab.

Critical: A security policy that nobody knows about is the same as not having one at all. All lab staff must be briefed about the security policy and any changes to it.

People working together on cases should have the same access level to ensure proper information sharing while maintaining security boundaries.

High-risk investigations demand more security than minimum lab requirements provide. They cannot be conducted in regular forensics lab facilities.

TEMPEST facilities are hardened so that electrical signals from computers, networks, and telephone systems cannot be easily monitored or accessed by someone outside. Computers emanate electromagnetic radiation (EMR) that can be picked up by specialized devices up to half a mile away.

TEMPEST facilities are very expensive. A less costly alternative is using low-emanation workstations that produce reduced electromagnetic emissions.

Evidence containers (also called evidence lockers) must be secure to prevent unauthorized access. Keep them in a restricted area, limit authorized access, and always lock containers when not in use.

Combination Locks

Provide the same security level for the combination as for the contents. Destroy previous combinations, allow only authorized personnel to change them, and change combinations every six months or when authorized personnel leave.

Keyed Padlocks

Appoint a key custodian, stamp sequential numbers on duplicate keys, maintain a registry, conduct monthly audits, and change locks and keys annually. Never use a master key for several locks.

Containers should be made of steel with an internal cabinet or external padlock. When possible, build an evidence storage room in your lab, acquire a media safe, and keep an updated evidence log.

Facility Maintenance

Immediately repair damages to walls, ceilings, and floors. Escort cleaning crews and monitor them while they work. Implement measures to protect against static electricity and maintain two separate trash containers — one for general materials and one for sensitive materials.

Security Tip: Dumpster diving is a technique social engineers use to obtain information from improperly disposed materials, including passwords, Social Security numbers, and credit card numbers. Always dispose of sensitive materials properly.

A strong physical security policy is essential. Enforcement mechanisms include:

Sign-in logs for visitors, escorting all visitors inside the lab, alerting others of visitor presence, installing an alarm system, and hiring guards to protect the premises, especially at night.

Auditing the Lab

Regular audits help enforce security policies. Audits should cover: the ceiling, floor, roof, and exterior walls; doors and door locks; visitor logs; evidence container logs; and ensuring all evidence is secured at the end of every workday.

Forensics labs come in various sizes and configurations. Key design considerations include number of workstations, workbenches, working area size, exit doors, evidence room layout, and communications options (phone, Internet, LAN, WAN).

Mid-size labs (typically private businesses) should have multiple workstations, at least two exits for safety, cubicles or separate offices to reinforce need-to-know policy, and library space for software/hardware storage.

Selecting Workstations

Workstations should match your budget, needs, and typical tasks. For police labs, the challenge is dealing with diverse hardware and software from the community. Consider creating a lightweight, mobile forensic workstation for imaging suspect drives in the field.

For private-sector labs, selection is usually easier since you deal with a more homogeneous environment and know exactly what platforms are in use.

Beyond workstations, labs need essential hardware peripherals: digital cameras, antistatic bags, IDE and SATA cables, USB cables, SCSI cards, graphics cards, FireWire and USB adapters, assorted hard drives, notebook drive adapters, and computer hand tools.

Software Inventory

Maintain licensed copies of: office suites (current and older versions), hexadecimal editors, programming languages, specialized image viewers, alternative office suites, and accounting applications.

Disaster Recovery Plan

A good plan covers: recovering from catastrophic situations, severe virus infections, workstation reconfigurations, backup policies for single disks and RAID servers, and configuration management tools to track software updates.

Equipment Upgrades

Risk management involves determining acceptable risk for any process. Computing equipment typically lasts 18 to 36 months under normal conditions — schedule upgrades accordingly. Identify equipment you depend on for periodic replacement and equipment you can replace when it fails.

The best way to get approval for a forensics lab is showing it will reduce costs and increase profits. Forensic investigations can help reduce litigation costs and better protect intellectual property, trade secrets, and business plans.

Stages of a Business Case

1. Justification → 2. Budget development → 3. Facility cost → 4. Hardware requirements → 5. Software requirements → 6. Miscellaneous budget needs → 7. Approval and acquisition → 8. Implementation → 9. Acceptance testing → 10. Correction for acceptance → 11. Production

Key Principle: The better you plan your business case, the more likely it will be accepted and funded. Include concrete examples of how forensic investigations will save the organization money.

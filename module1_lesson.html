Digital forensics is the application of computer science and investigative procedures for a legal purpose involving the analysis of digital evidence after proper search authority, chain of custody, validation with mathematics, use of validated tools, repeatability, reporting, and possible expert presentation.

In October 2012, ISO 27037 was ratified as the international standard for digital forensics. The Federal Rules of Evidence (FRE), signed into law in 1973, ensure consistency across federal proceedings.

Key Milestone: The FBI Computer Analysis and Response Team (CART) was formed in 1984 to handle growing digital evidence caseloads.

The Fourth Amendment to the U.S. Constitution protects everyone's right to be secure from unreasonable search and seizure.

Public-Sector: Government agencies handling criminal cases. Must follow strict legal guidelines including the Fourth Amendment. Criminal cases progress through three stages: complaint, investigation, and prosecution.

Key roles: Digital Evidence First Responder (DEFR) and Digital Evidence Specialist (DES). An affidavit is a sworn statement submitted to a judge to request a search warrant.

Private-Sector: Companies and lawyers addressing policy violations and litigation. Governed by internal policies, not directly by criminal law. Corporate crimes include e-mail harassment, data falsification, embezzlement, sabotage, and industrial espionage.

Your credibility hinges on professional conduct â€” ethics, morals, and objectivity. Keep cases confidential.

Critical: The chain of custody is the documented trail evidence takes from discovery to court. A broken chain can invalidate your entire case.

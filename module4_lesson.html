Digital evidence is any information stored or transmitted in digital form. U.S. courts accept digital evidence as physical evidence, meaning digital data is treated as a tangible object.

Groups such as the Scientific Working Group on Digital Evidence (SWGDE) set standards for recovering, preserving, and examining digital evidence.

General tasks investigators perform when working with digital evidence include: identify digital artifacts that can be used as evidence, collect, preserve, and document evidence, analyze, identify, and organize evidence, and rebuild or repeat a situation to verify results can be reproduced reliably.

Key Point: Collecting computers and processing a criminal or incident scene must be done systematically. An unstructured approach risks losing or contaminating vital evidence.

Consistent practices help verify your work and enhance your credibility. You should comply with your state’s rules of evidence or with the Federal Rules of Evidence (FRE). Evidence admitted in a criminal case can also be used in a civil suit, and vice versa.

Digital evidence is unlike other physical evidence because it can be changed more easily. The only way to detect these changes is to compare the original data with a duplicate.

Hearsay & Business Records

Hearsay is secondhand or indirect evidence. However, the business-record exception allows “records of regularly conducted activity” such as business memos, reports, and data compilations to be admitted.

Computer records fall into two categories: computer-generated records (system logs, proxy server logs) and computer-stored records (spreadsheets, documents created by a person). Both must be shown to be authentic and trustworthy to be admitted into evidence.

Best Evidence Rule

The best evidence rule states that to prove the content of a written document, recording, or photograph, the original is ordinarily required. The FRE allows duplicates when produced by the same impression as the original. Bit-stream copies created and maintained properly can be admitted in court, though they are not considered best evidence.

Private-sector organizations include small to medium businesses, large corporations, and non-government organizations (NGOs). NGOs must comply with state public disclosure and federal Freedom of Information Act (FOIA) laws.

ISPs and other communication companies can investigate computer abuse by their employees but not by customers, except for emergency situations.

Investigating corporate incidents is generally easier than criminal scenes because the workplace is controlled and businesses typically have inventory databases of hardware and software.

Warning Banners: Companies should display a warning banner or publish a policy stating they reserve the right to inspect computing assets at will. This allows investigators to conduct covert surveillance with little or no cause.

When a Corporate Case Becomes Criminal

If you discover evidence of a crime during a policy investigation: 1) Determine whether the incident meets elements of criminal law. 2) Inform management. 3) Stop your investigation to avoid violating Fourth Amendment restrictions. 4) Work with the corporate attorney to respond to police requests.

You must be familiar with criminal rules of search and seizure. Probable cause is the standard specifying whether a police officer has the right to make an arrest, conduct a search, or obtain a warrant.

With probable cause, an officer can obtain a search warrant from a judge authorizing seizure of specific evidence related to a criminal complaint. The Fourth Amendment states that only warrants “particularly describing the place to be searched, and the persons or things to be seized” can be issued.

Warrant Concepts & Terms

Innocent information is unrelated data often mixed in with the evidence you seek. A limiting phrase in the warrant allows police to separate innocent information from evidence.

The plain view doctrine states that objects in plain view of an officer who has the legal right to be in position to see them are subject to seizure without a warrant. Three criteria must be met: the officer is where they have a legal right to be, ordinary senses are not enhanced by advanced technology, and any discovery must be by chance.

Digital Limitation: The plain view doctrine’s applicability to digital forensics is increasingly being rejected. If an examiner finds unrelated illegal material (e.g., finding child exploitation material while searching for drug evidence), they must obtain an additional warrant before continuing that new search.

Preparing for a computer search and seizure is probably the most important step in computing investigations. You may need answers from the victim and an informant (a detective, witness, or coworker of the person of interest).

Key Preparation Steps

Identify the nature of the case — Determine whether it involves the private or public sector, which dictates how you proceed.

Identify the OS or digital device — Estimate storage size, number of devices to process, and what hardware is involved (PCs, smartphones, tablets, laptops).

Determine if you can seize the computers — Law enforcement needs a warrant. If removing computers would irreparably harm a business, they should not be taken offsite. Cloud storage adds further complications.

Get a detailed description of the location — Identify potential HAZMAT (hazardous materials) situations. Use HAZMAT bags for evidence in contaminated environments.

Determine who is in charge — Private-sector cases usually need one person; law enforcement designates lead investigators for large-scale operations.

Use additional technical expertise — Specialists in specific OSs, RAID servers, or databases may be needed. They must be trained in investigative techniques to avoid damaging evidence.

Field Kits

Prepare an initial-response field kit (lightweight, portable, minimum tools for disk acquisition) and an extensive-response field kit (all tools you can afford to take).

The two major goals of securing the scene: preserve evidence integrity and keep information confidential. Create a security perimeter using barrier tape and enforce legal authority.

Watch Out: Professional curiosity — police officers and other professionals not on the crime scene team who want to look around — is a common cause of evidence loss or corruption.

The Automated Fingerprint Identification System (AFIS) can take elimination prints of everyone who had access to the scene.

Scene Processing Guidelines

Keep a journal • Secure the scene and be courteous with onlookers • Take video and still recordings • Sketch the scene • Check the state of computers immediately • Don’t cut power to a running system (except older Windows 9x/MS-DOS) • Save data from current applications safely • Record all active windows or shell sessions • Close applications and shut down the computer properly.

Bag and Tag Evidence

1) Assign one person to collect and log all evidence. 2) Tag everything with date, time, serial numbers, make/model, and collector’s name. 3) Maintain two separate logs. 4) Maintain constant control of collected evidence. 5) Look for passwords, PINs, and bank account information. 6) Collect all documentation, hardware, software, and media.

Creating Image Files

1) Copy all image files to a large drive or SAN (Storage Area Network). 2) Start your forensics tool to analyze the evidence. 3) Run an MD5 or SHA-1 hashing algorithm to get a digital hash. 4) Secure the original media in an evidence locker.

Storage Media Options

CDs/DVDs: Lifespan of 2–5 years. Solid-state USB drives: Currently the optimum choice — more durable. Magnetic tapes (4mm DAT): 40–72 GB capacity, 30-year lifespan, but slow. Super DLT: Designed for RAID backups, can store over 1 TB.

Best Practice: Don’t rely on one media storage method. Make two copies of every image using different tools to prevent data loss.

Evidence Retention & Documentation

Restrict access to your lab and evidence storage. Maintain a sign-in roster for all visitors. An evidence custody form identifies the evidence, who handled it, and dates/times of handling. Include MD5 and SHA-1 hash values. Use antistatic bags for electronic components.

Cyclic Redundancy Check (CRC) determines whether a file’s contents have changed. CRC-32 is the most recent version. CRC is not considered a forensic hashing algorithm.

Message Digest 5 (MD5) translates a file, folder, or entire drive into a hexadecimal hash value. If a single bit changes, the hash value changes.

Three Rules for Forensic Hashes

1) You can’t predict the hash value of a file or device. 2) No two hash values can be the same. 3) If anything changes in the file or device, the hash value must change.

SHA-1 (Secure Hash Algorithm version 1) was developed by NIST (National Institute of Standards and Technology). Both MD5 and SHA-1 collisions have occurred.

A nonkeyed hash set is generated by a software tool (e.g., the Linux md5sum command). A keyed hash set is created using an encryption utility’s secret key.

General Case Tasks

1) Identify the case requirements • 2) Plan your investigation • 3) Conduct the investigation • 4) Complete the case report • 5) Critique the case.

Civil vs. Criminal Investigations

Most corporate cases are low-level investigations (noncriminal). Common activities include recovering evidence like PST email files, using covert surveillance (must be defined in company policy), and sniffing tools for monitoring data transmissions.

Criminal investigations may involve fraud, check fraud, or homicides. A warrant is required to seize evidence, and the search area must be limited.

Sparse Acquisition

Sparse acquisition extracts only data related to your case from allocated files on large systems. Its drawback is that it does not recover data in free or slack space.

The cloud has introduced ways of managing data that didn't exist a decade ago, creating unique challenges for forensic investigators. Cloud computing is defined by NIST as a computing storage system that provides on-demand network access for multiple users and can allocate storage to keep up with changing needs.

History of the Cloud

The concept of cloud computing originated with Professor John McCarthy of MIT and Dr. J.C.R. Licklider, director at the U.S. Department of Defense ARPA. In 1999, Salesforce.com developed a Web service applying digital marketing research to business subscribers, which eventually led the way to cloud computing. In 2002, Amazon created Mechanical Turk and launched EC2 in 2006. After Web 2.0 in 2009, other providers launched services including Google Apps, Apple iCloud, and Microsoft OneDrive.

üí° Cloud computing has evolved from a theoretical concept in the 1960s to a multi-billion-dollar industry powering the majority of modern business operations.

Four Deployment Methods

Public ‚Äî accessible to anyone. Private ‚Äî accessed only by credentialed users. Community ‚Äî shared service for a specific purpose. Hybrid ‚Äî combines public, private, or community services under one cloud with data segregation.

Cloud forensics applies digital forensics to cloud computing and is considered a subset of network forensics. It has three dimensions:

Organizational ‚Äî addresses the structure of the cloud. Legal ‚Äî covers service agreements and jurisdictional matters. Technical ‚Äî deals with procedures and specialized applications for forensic recovery and analysis.

Required Tool Capabilities

Forensic data collection ‚Äî identify, label, record, and acquire data. Elastic, static, and live forensics ‚Äî expand and contract storage capabilities. Evidence segregation ‚Äî handle shared environments. Virtualized environment investigation ‚Äî examine virtual systems.

Service Level Agreements (SLAs)

Cloud Service Agreements (CSAs) are contracts between a CSP and the customer describing what services are provided and at what level. They should specify service hours, restrictions, availability, support levels, response times, throughput limitations, incident response plans, disaster recovery, fees, and security measures.

Forensic examiners should be most concerned with restrictions applied to customers and security measures. Examiners should also review a CSP's policies, standards, guidelines, and processes/procedures.

Jurisdiction Issues

There is currently no law ensuring uniform access or required handling procedures for the cloud. Investigators should be concerned about commingled data ‚Äî cases where relevant data is mixed with unrelated customer data. The EU Directive 95/46/EC is more restrictive than US rules regarding private information for EU citizens. Digital forensics examiners could be held liable when conducting cloud investigations ‚Äî always consult legal experts.

Architecture

No two CSPs are configured exactly the same way. Customer data may be commingled, and most CSPs keep storage locations secret. Differences in recording procedures and log keeping complicate the chain of evidence.

Analysis of Cloud Forensic Data

Analyzing evidence from a cloud requires verifying data with other data and log records. Examiners compare MAC dates (Modified, last Access, and Created). Metadata should be examined to validate file accesses.

Anti-Forensics

Anti-forensics is the act of destroying electronically stored information (ESI) that is potential evidence. Hackers may: insert malware in other files, use encryption to obfuscate malware, use data-hiding utilities that append malware to existing files, or change timestamps to disrupt timelines. Calculating hash values and comparing them with known good files helps identify altered files.

Incident First Responders & Role Management

CSPs typically have trained personnel to respond to network incidents. When they don't, the forensic examiner organizes CSP staff. Key concerns include staff cooperation, management authority, operations security briefings, and evidence collection training.

Role management covers data owners, identity protection, users, and access controls ‚Äî useful for identifying additional victims or suspects.

Virtual environmentsSnapshots before, during, and after incident

With virtual environments, snapshots provide valuable information. Examiners should re-create separate servers from each snapshot, acquire images, and calculate hashes for all files.

Encryption in the Cloud

Many CSPs offer encryption services. Encrypted data exists in two states: Data at rest (written to disk) and Data in motion (transmitted over a network). Some systems also encrypt data in use (in RAM). If encrypted data is encountered, find out from the CSP what type of encryption was used. If the data owner is uncooperative, turn to attorneys or the data owner's management.

üí° Emerging technologies: Homomorphic encryption uses an "ideal lattice" mathematical formula, while blockchain technology traces information while keeping it secure.

Investigating CSPs

Key questions investigators should ask: Does the investigator have authority to use cloud staff and resources? Is detailed knowledge of the topology, policies, and storage methods available? Are there restrictions on collecting evidence from remote storage? Is the data commingled with other customers' data? Is the data local or remote, and can the CSP provide a forensically sound connection?

Investigating Cloud Customers

Locations to check on a customer's computer or mobile device: Web browser cache, the CSP application folder (under the user's account), and the Windows Prefetch folder.

Understanding Prefetch Files

Prefetch files contain DLL pathnames and metadata used by applications, including MAC times in UTC format and a counter of how many times the application has run. If deleted, Windows re-creates one that resets MAC times and the counter.

Examining Cloud Data on a PC

Three widely used cloud services ‚Äî Dropbox (base-64 format since 2012), Google Drive (key files: sync_config.db, snapshot.db, sync_log.log), and OneDrive (SyncEngine and SyncDiagnostics logs). Each stores user data in specific application and log file locations that can reveal file transfers and access history.

Few tools are specifically designed for cloud forensics, but many digital, network, and e-discovery tools can be combined to collect and analyze cloud data.

People store a wealth of information on cell phones, yet most do not think about securing their devices. Investigating smartphones and mobile devices is one of the most challenging tasks in digital forensics because no single standard exists for how phones store data, and new models come out approximately every six months.

Data Stored on Mobile Devices

CategoryExamples
CommunicationsIncoming, outgoing, and missed calls; MMS and SMS messages; e-mail accounts; instant messaging logs
MediaPictures, videos, music files, voice recordings and voicemail
Personal DataCalendars, address books, social media account information, bank account logins
LocationGPS data, cell tower connections, Wi-Fi access point history
Web ActivityWeb pages visited, browser history, cached data
Legal Note: A search warrant is needed to examine mobile devices because they can contain so much personal information. The Supreme Court ruled in Riley v. California (2014) that police generally need a warrant to search cell phones.

Mobile phone technology has advanced rapidly through multiple generations, each bringing faster speeds and new capabilities for both users and forensic investigators.

Network Standards

CDMA (Code Division Multiple Access) uses spread-spectrum modulation. GSM (Global System for Mobile Communications) uses the TDMA (Time Division Multiple Access) technique, where multiple phones take turns sharing a channel. The ITU (International Telecommunication Union) developed the 3G standard.

Cell Communication Components

Three main components enable cell communication: the Base Transceiver Station (BTS) which communicates directly with phones, the Base Station Controller (BSC) which manages multiple BTS units, and the Mobile Switching Center (MSC) which connects calls to the wider telephone network.

Mobile devices range from simple phones to powerful smartphones, tablets, and smartwatches. Understanding their internal architecture is critical for forensic examination.

Hardware Components

Cell phone hardware consists of a microprocessor, ROM, RAM, a digital signal processor, a radio module, a microphone and speaker, hardware interfaces, and an LCD display.

Memory and Storage

Phones store system data in EEPROM (Electronically Erasable Programmable Read-Only Memory), which enables service providers to reprogram phones without physically accessing memory chips. The OS is stored in ROM (nonvolatile memory), available even if the phone loses power.

SIM Cards

Subscriber Identity Module (SIM) cards are usually found in GSM devices and consist of a microprocessor and internal memory. GSM refers to mobile phones as “mobile stations” and divides them into two parts: the SIM card and the mobile equipment (ME).

SIM Card FunctionDescription
Network IdentificationIdentifies the subscriber to the cellular network
Service StorageStores service-related information
Device BackupCan be used to back up device data

SIM cards come in three sizes (standard, micro, nano). Their portability of information is what makes them so versatile. Older CDMA phones do not use SIM cards — they incorporate the card’s functions directly into the phone. Many modern phones also include SD cards for external storage.

Peripheral Memory Cards

Memory cards that may be found in mobile devices include: Compact Flash (CF), MultiMediaCard (MMC), and Secure Digital (SD).

The main concerns when seizing mobile devices are loss of power, synchronization with cloud services, and remote wiping. All mobile devices have volatile memory, so maintaining power before retrieving RAM data is critical.

Immediate Seizure Steps

Any mobile device attached to a PC via USB should be disconnected immediately to prevent automatic synchronization that might overwrite data. Depending on the warrant or subpoena, the time of seizure may be relevant, as messages might continue to arrive.

Signal Isolation Methods

MethodProsCons
Airplane ModeQuick, preserves powerRequires touching the device interface
Paint CanSimple RF shieldingLimited effectiveness, not forensically standard
Faraday BagPurpose-built RF shieldingAccelerates battery drain (roaming mode)
Power OffStops all communicationMay trigger encryption; loss of volatile data
Critical Warning: Isolating a device from signals puts it into roaming mode, which accelerates battery drainage. If the device is on and unlocked, isolate it from the network and disable the screen lock. If it’s on and locked, your options vary by device type. If the device is off, attempt a physical static acquisition first.

Four Areas to Check in the Lab

Back in the forensics lab, examine these four areas: internal memory, the SIM card, removable/external memory cards, and the network provider (requires a search warrant or subpoena). Cloud backups stored by carriers or third parties add additional complexity.

Retrievable Information Categories

Information from SIM cards falls into four categories: service-related data (identifiers for SIM and subscriber), call data (numbers dialed), message information, and location information. The SIM file system uses a hierarchical structure. If power has been lost, PINs or access codes may be required.

Mobile forensics is an evolving science. The biggest challenge is dealing with constantly changing models of cell phones.

Steps to Analyze a Mobile Device

The procedure follows these steps: identify the mobile device, install the appropriate forensics software on your workstation, attach the phone to power and connect the correct cables, then start the forensics software and begin downloading available information.

SIM Card Reader Procedure

A SIM card reader is a combination hardware/software device. You need to work in a lab equipped with appropriate antistatic devices. The general procedure: remove the device’s back panel, remove the battery, remove the SIM card from its holder, and insert it into the card reader. Documenting unread messages before any extraction is critical.

Common Mobile Forensics Tools

Software tools differ in the information they display and their level of detail. Some tools are designed for updating files, not retrieving data — so they may not be forensically sound. Every program has its idiosyncrasies, and investigators must understand the shortcomings of their tools.

Key Tip: You can also connect a mobile device directly to a computer via USB to browse the file system. This method requires a USB write-blocker and is not ideal, but it allows gathering basic information when specialized tools are unavailable.

Advanced mobile forensic tools are widely used by law enforcement. These tools allow you to determine the device’s make and model, learn what must be done before connecting the device, and then retrieve the data.

Extraction Options

Typical extraction options include: Logical (user data like contacts, SMS, call logs), File System (full directory structure including hidden files), and Physical (bit-by-bit copy including deleted data and unallocated space).

Direct Computer Connection

You can also connect a mobile device to a computer to browse the file system. For example, an Android device connected by USB cable shows its directory structure including folders like Internal Storage, DCIM, Downloads, Music, Pictures, and more. To make a forensic acquisition this way, you need a USB write-blocker.

Many mobile forensics tools are available, but most are not free. Methods and techniques for acquiring evidence will change as the mobile device market continues to expand. Subscribe to user groups and professional organizations to stay current.

The Internet of Things (IoT) has evolved into the Internet of Everything (IoE) and now the Internet of Anything (IoA), each bringing new forensic challenges.

The Evolution

IoT encompasses devices that connect to the Internet — more than the number of people on Earth, expected to reach 50 billion devices in coming decades. IoE adds intangible features like search engines and streaming platforms. IoA includes cars, homes, pets, livestock, and the applications that make them work together.

5G Device Categories

CategoryFull NamePurpose
eMBBEnhanced Mobile BroadbandHigh-speed data for video and AR/VR

New Forensic Challenges

5G devices introduce new communication patterns: People-to-Device (P2D), Device-to-Device (D2D), and Device-to-Cloud (D2C) communications. Each creates new forensic evidence sources and challenges.

Wearable computers (smartwatches, fitness trackers) will pose many new challenges for investigators, containing health data, location history, and communication logs. Vehicle system forensics addresses the many sensors in modern cars, which can contain GPS logs, dashcam footage, Bluetooth pairing history, and more.

BYOD Challenges: Bring Your Own Device (BYOD) practices complicate forensics by mixing personal and business data on the same device. Type 2 hypervisors for mobile devices separate this data but add another layer of complexity to forensic investigations.
